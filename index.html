Understanding the Interconnected Crisis

Four dimensions tell one story: a planet warming at an unprecedented rate, with profound consequences for human civilization

The 21st century will be defined by humanity's relationship with a rapidly warming planet. Since 1880, the global average surface temperature has risen by approximately 1.3°C, with the rate of warming roughly doubling since 1980. The last decade was the warmest in at least 125,000 years. This is not a natural cycle — the concentration of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere, now exceeding 420 parts per million, is higher than at any point in at least three million years, and the isotopic fingerprint of that carbon unambiguously identifies fossil fuel combustion as its source.

But temperature is only one dimension of the story. The human consequences — who is exposed, where, and at what cost — are what transform abstract climate data into an urgent policy challenge. This analysis examines four interconnected dimensions: the physical warming of the planet, the accumulation of heat-trapping gases driving that warming, the billions of people facing lethal heat conditions as a result, and the mounting economic costs that will reshape global prosperity. The projections shown follow the IPCC's Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs). The gap between these scenarios after 2050 illustrates the stakes: every tenth of a degree of avoided warming means tens of millions of people spared from lethal heat exposure and trillions of dollars in avoided economic losses. The choices made in this decade will echo through the rest of the century and beyond.

Global Temperature Anomaly
NASA GISTEMP v4 observations with IPCC SSP projections to 2100
2024

Global mean surface temperature is the most fundamental measure of climate change. NASA's GISTEMP dataset, maintained by the Goddard Institute for Space Studies, combines land surface air temperatures from weather stations with sea surface temperatures from ships and buoys to produce a continuous record stretching back to 1880. The baseline period — 1951 to 1980 — represents a relatively stable pre-acceleration climate against which all anomalies are measured.

The solid orange line traces observed warming: a gradual rise through the early 20th century, a mid-century plateau, and then a sharp acceleration from the 1970s onward that continues unabated. The year 2024 was the warmest on record at +1.28°C above the baseline, and the ten warmest years have all occurred since 2014. The dashed lines represent two futures from the IPCC's Shared Socioeconomic Pathways. SSP2-4.5 — the "middle of the road" scenario — assumes emissions peak around 2050 before declining, limiting warming to approximately 2.7°C by century's end. SSP5-8.5 — the fossil-fueled development pathway — would drive warming beyond 4.4°C, a level not seen on Earth in at least 15 million years.

The horizontal reference lines mark the Paris Agreement thresholds of 1.5°C and 2.0°C. The 1.5°C line was first breached in 2016 (an El Niño year), and the long-term average is now approaching this threshold. The difference between 1.5°C and 2.0°C may sound small, but it represents roughly double the coral reef destruction, double the frequency of extreme heat events, and an additional 10 centimetres of sea level rise by 2100. The divergence of the two scenarios after 2050 illustrates what is arguably the most consequential policy choice in human history.

Atmospheric CO₂ Concentration
NOAA GML measurements with IPCC Shared Socioeconomic Pathway projections
2024

Atmospheric carbon dioxide is the primary driver of long-term climate change. Measured continuously at the Mauna Loa Observatory in Hawaii since 1958 — producing the famous Keeling Curve — and extended backward through ice core analysis, the CO₂ record tells an unambiguous story. Pre-industrial concentrations held steady at approximately 280 parts per million for thousands of years. The industrial revolution ignited a relentless climb: 300 ppm was passed around 1910, 350 ppm — long considered the "safe" upper boundary by climate scientists — was breached in 1988, and 420 ppm was reached in 2023, a level last seen during the Pliocene epoch some three million years ago, when sea levels were 15–25 metres higher and forests grew in the Arctic.

The two SSP scenarios diverge dramatically. Under SSP2-4.5, CO₂ stabilizes around 550 ppm by 2100 as the world transitions toward cleaner energy and sustainable land use. Under SSP5-8.5 — essentially a continuation of historical emission trends without meaningful mitigation — concentrations soar past 1,100 ppm, a level not seen since the Eocene epoch 50 million years ago, when the planet was entirely ice-free. The 350 ppm and 450 ppm reference lines mark thresholds beyond which scientists warn of escalating risks of tipping points: ice sheet collapse, permafrost carbon release, and Amazon dieback.

Every tonne of CO₂ emitted adds to the atmospheric stock, where it persists for centuries. The relationship is cumulative: even if emissions stopped tomorrow, the CO₂ already in the atmosphere would continue warming the planet for decades. This is why the rate of emission reduction matters as much as the eventual net-zero date — the area under the curve (cumulative emissions) determines peak warming, not the year net-zero is achieved.

Population Exposed to Extreme Heat
Millions of people facing wet-bulb temperatures exceeding 35°C for 15+ days per year
2024

Extreme heat exposure is measured here using wet-bulb temperature (TW) — a metric that combines heat and humidity to assess the human body's capacity to cool itself through sweating. At TW ≥ 35°C, even a perfectly healthy person resting in shade with unlimited access to water will suffer heat stroke within a matter of hours. This is a hard physiological limit: the body simply cannot shed heat faster than it accumulates it when the surrounding air is both hot enough and humid enough. Unlike dry heat, which can be mitigated with shade and hydration, extreme wet-bulb conditions are unsurvivable without artificial cooling.

The stacked areas show the regional distribution of exposed populations. South Asia — encompassing India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, and Sri Lanka — faces by far the heaviest burden, driven by the lethal combination of intense pre-monsoon heat, high humidity from the Bay of Bengal and Arabian Sea, and dense populations with limited access to air conditioning. Sub-Saharan Africa follows, where rapid population growth in the Sahel and West Africa intersects with rising temperatures. Southeast Asia, the Middle East and North Africa, and Latin America each face growing exposure. By mid-century, between 2.6 and 3.5 billion people may live in zones that experience lethal wet-bulb conditions for at least 15 days annually.

The injustice is stark: the regions facing the most severe heat exposure are, overwhelmingly, those that contributed the least to cumulative greenhouse gas emissions. South Asia's historical contribution to atmospheric CO₂ is less than 3% of the global total; Sub-Saharan Africa's is under 2%. The populations bearing the brunt of extreme heat did not cause the problem — they inherited it. This asymmetry between responsibility and impact is one of the defining moral challenges of the 21st century, and it underpins the increasingly urgent calls for adaptation finance and loss and damage funding from wealthy, high-emitting nations to the most vulnerable communities.

Cumulative Economic Damage
Annual GDP loss from climate damages with cumulative costs in trillions USD
2024

The economic costs of climate change are modelled here using a quadratic damage function — a well-established approach in climate economics, pioneered by Marshall Burke, Solomon Hsiang, and colleagues, and incorporated into the IPCC's Sixth Assessment Report. The core insight: damages do not rise linearly with temperature. A 2°C world is not simply twice as costly as a 1°C world — it is approximately four times as costly, because impacts compound and interact across sectors. Agricultural yields decline nonlinearly with heat; labour productivity falls sharply above certain temperature thresholds; infrastructure designed for historical climate norms fails under unprecedented stress; and the costs of adaptation themselves consume an increasing share of economic output.

The red-brown bars show annual GDP loss as a percentage of global output — the drag that climate change imposes each year relative to a no-warming counterfactual. The gold line tracks cumulative damages in trillions of US dollars, summing the annual losses from 2020 onward. By mid-century, annual losses could reach 3–5% of global GDP under moderate scenarios; by 2100, the range extends to 10–18% under higher-warming pathways. To put this in perspective: the 2008 global financial crisis caused a one-time GDP loss of approximately 0.1% when smoothed over a decade. Climate change represents a permanent, compounding drag that grows larger every year.

The implications for investment and policy are profound. The estimated cost of achieving net-zero emissions by 2050 — including the full transformation of energy, transport, industrial, and agricultural systems — is in the range of 1–2% of global GDP annually. The damages from inaction, as the chart shows, far exceed this figure by mid-century and accelerate thereafter. In economic terms, climate mitigation is not a cost — it is an investment with an extraordinarily high rate of return, measured in avoided damages, preserved ecosystems, and lives protected from extreme heat.

Why This Matters

Four dimensions, one interconnected crisis

The Physics

For every tonne of CO₂ emitted, the planet traps additional heat. Since 1880, global mean temperature has risen ~1.3°C. The rate of warming since 1970 is unprecedented in at least 2,000 years. The last time CO₂ exceeded 400 ppm (the Pliocene, ~3 million years ago), sea levels were 15–25 metres higher.

The Human Cost

Wet-bulb temperature (TW) combines heat and humidity. At TW ≥ 35°C, even a healthy person resting in shade with water will suffer heat stroke within hours. This threshold has already been breached in parts of South Asia, the Persian Gulf, and Mexico — decades earlier than models projected.

The Pathways

The divergence between SSP2-4.5 and SSP5-8.5 after 2050 is stark. Under the moderate scenario, emissions peak mid-century and warming limits to ~2.7°C. Under continued fossil dependence, warming reaches ~4.4°C. Every fraction of a degree means hundreds of millions fewer people facing lethal heat.

The Solutions

The technologies exist: rapid clean energy deployment, urban cooling, early warning systems, heat-resistant crops, adaptation finance. The challenge is diffusion — how fast proven solutions reach the populations that need them most. This is the central research question of H Heuristics.

What Can Be Done

From adaptation to mitigation — the tools exist, the challenge is deployment

Clean Energy at Speed and Scale

The cost of solar photovoltaic electricity has fallen by over 90% since 2010; onshore wind by over 70%. Battery storage costs have declined by a similar magnitude. The technologies needed to decarbonize the global power system are not only available — they are now the cheapest source of new electricity in most of the world. The challenge is no longer invention but deployment: building transmission infrastructure, reforming permitting processes, training workforces, and ensuring that the transition is just and equitable across communities that depend on fossil fuel economies.

Urban Cooling and Heat Adaptation

Cities are heat amplifiers — the urban heat island effect can add 5–10°C to local temperatures. Solutions include cool roofs and pavements that reflect rather than absorb solar radiation, urban tree canopy expansion, green corridors for natural ventilation, and community cooling centres for the most vulnerable. These are proven, cost-effective interventions. Singapore's extensive greening programme has reduced urban temperatures by as much as 4°C; Ahmedabad's heat action plan in India has saved an estimated 1,100 lives per year since its implementation in 2013.

Early Warning and Health Systems

Heatwaves are predictable — meteorology can now forecast extreme heat events with 5–10 days of lead time. Coupling these forecasts with public health protocols (cooling centre activation, hospital surge capacity, outreach to elderly and isolated populations) dramatically reduces mortality. The Lancet Countdown estimates that a global heat-health warning system covering the most vulnerable 50% of the world's population would cost approximately $1 per person per year — one of the highest-return investments in public health available today.

Adaptation Finance and Climate Justice

The countries most exposed to extreme heat — in South Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, and the Middle East — have contributed less than 5% of cumulative historical emissions. The principle of climate justice demands that wealthy, high-emitting nations provide financial and technical support for adaptation in the most vulnerable regions. The UN Environment Programme estimates the annual adaptation finance gap at $194–366 billion. Closing this gap is not charity — it is an investment in global stability, reduced migration pressure, and the shared infrastructure of a liveable planet.
