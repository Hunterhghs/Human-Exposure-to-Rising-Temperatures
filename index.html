About This Visualization

Understanding the four interconnected dimensions of the climate crisis

🌡️ Temperature Anomaly

Global mean surface temperature relative to the 1951–1980 baseline. The solid line shows NASA GISTEMP v4 observations. Dashed lines represent two IPCC scenarios: SSP2-4.5 (moderate emissions, ~2.7°C by 2100) and SSP5-8.5 (high emissions, ~4.4°C by 2100). The Paris Agreement thresholds of 1.5°C and 2.0°C are marked.

🫁 Carbon Dioxide

Atmospheric CO₂ concentration in parts per million, from ice core reconstructions and direct Mauna Loa Observatory measurements. The pre-industrial baseline was ~280 ppm. We crossed 420 ppm in 2023 — a level not seen in at least 3 million years. The 350 ppm and 450 ppm lines mark key risk thresholds identified by climate science.

👥 Extreme Heat Exposure

Number of people exposed to extreme heat conditions — defined as wet-bulb temperatures exceeding 35°C for more than 15 days per year. At this threshold, the human body cannot cool itself through sweating, even in shade. The stacked areas show the regional breakdown: South Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, Southeast Asia, the Middle East, Latin America, and the rest of the world.

💰 Economic Damage

Annual GDP loss from climate damages as a percentage of global economic output, based on quadratic damage functions from Burke et al. and IPCC AR6 WG2. The bars show yearly impact; the gold line tracks cumulative damages in trillions of USD. Even under moderate scenarios, climate change imposes a growing drag on the global economy that compounds over decades.

Why This Matters

The middle of the 21st century will be defined by human exposure to heat

📊 The Numbers

By 2050, an estimated 2.6–3.5 billion people will live in zones experiencing lethal heat conditions for at least 15 days per year. The vast majority are in South Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, and the Middle East — regions that contributed least to cumulative emissions.

The economic cost of inaction could reach 10–18% of global GDP by 2100, dwarfing the investments needed for clean energy transition and adaptation.

🔬 The Science

Wet-bulb temperature (TW) combines heat and humidity to measure the human body's ability to cool itself. At TW ≥ 35°C, even a healthy person resting in shade with unlimited water will suffer heat stroke within hours.

This threshold has already been breached briefly in parts of South Asia, the Persian Gulf, and Mexico — decades earlier than climate models had projected.

🛤️ The Pathways

The divergence between SSP2-4.5 and SSP5-8.5 after 2050 represents one of the most consequential choices in human history. Under SSP2-4.5, emissions peak around mid-century and decline, limiting warming to ~2.7°C. Under SSP5-8.5, continued fossil fuel dependence drives warming toward 4.4°C or more.

Every fraction of a degree matters: the difference between 2.0°C and 3.0°C means hundreds of millions fewer people facing lethal heat.

💡 What Can Be Done

The solutions exist: rapid deployment of clean energy, urban cooling infrastructure, early warning systems, heat-resistant crop varieties, and targeted adaptation finance. The challenge is one of diffusion — how fast can proven technologies reach the populations that need them most?

This is the central question of H Heuristics' research: understanding the patterns, barriers, and accelerators of technology diffusion in a warming world.
